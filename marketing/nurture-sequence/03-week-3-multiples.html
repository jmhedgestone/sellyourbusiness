Last week we covered SDE — the earnings number that drives valuation. This week: the multiplier that gets applied to it.

Two businesses with $500K SDE will sell for very different prices depending on industry. Quick benchmarks (real ranges, not internet averages):

IndustryTypical SDE multiple
SaaS / recurring software4.0× – 8.0×
Professional services (CPA, dental, legal)2.5× – 5.0×
HVAC / plumbing / electrical3.0× – 5.0×
E-commerce (Amazon FBA, DTC)2.5× – 4.5×
Restaurants (independent)1.5× – 2.5×
Construction / trades (project-based)2.0× – 3.5×
Manufacturing3.5× – 6.0×

That spread isn't random. It tracks five things buyers care about:

  1. Recurring revenue %. SaaS gets 6–8× because subscriptions are predictable. Restaurants get 2× because last week's traffic doesn't predict next week's.
  2. Owner dependence. If the business runs without you for 30 days, multiples go up 0.5–1.5×. If everything depends on you, they go down.
  3. Customer concentration. One client = 40% of revenue? Cut your multiple by ~1.0×.
  4. Growth trajectory. 3 years of growing SDE adds 0.5–1.5× over a flat business.
  5. Industry headwinds. Brick-and-mortar retail loses to e-commerce. Trade businesses gain from boomer retirements.

What this means practically:

The single biggest valuation lever you control isn't growing revenue — it's reducing owner dependence in the 12 months before listing.

I've watched a $400K SDE service business go from a 2.8× multiple to a 4.5× multiple by cross-training a manager and removing the owner from daily ops. That's $680K in extra sale price for hiring one operations manager.
