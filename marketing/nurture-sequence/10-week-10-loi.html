The Letter of Intent (LOI) is non-binding, looks short, and is signed in the energy of "we have a deal!" That's exactly why it's where most sellers get fleeced.

Once you sign an LOI, you typically grant the buyer 60–120 days of exclusivity. During that window you can't talk to other buyers. The buyer knows this. So whatever they do during diligence — re-trading the price, bolting on conditions, dragging out timelines — happens with you over a barrel.

Here's what to negotiate hard before you sign:

1️⃣ Purchase price — and what's included

Specific dollar figure, not a range. Stock sale or asset sale (huge tax implications). Working capital target nailed down — "delivered with normal working capital" is a six-figure ambiguity.

2️⃣ Earn-out structure (if any)

Earn-outs are how buyers retroactively renegotiate the price. If there's an earn-out, the metrics must be: simple, controllable by you (not buyer-decided), capped, and time-limited. Better: no earn-out at all. Cash at close is king.

3️⃣ Exclusivity period — keep it tight

30–45 days, not 90+. Tied to specific buyer milestones (financing commitment, QoE complete). If they miss them, exclusivity drops and you can talk to backup buyers.

4️⃣ No-shop carve-outs

You should retain the right to respond to unsolicited inbound offers. Buyers will push back. Hold firm.

5️⃣ Reps & warranties — flag the scope

The LOI doesn't have the actual reps, but it should establish what's "customary." If the buyer wants extensive R&W with long survival periods, that's a future fight you should preview now.

6️⃣ Seller financing / rollover equity

If the buyer wants you to finance 20% or roll equity into the new entity — that's not a sale at the headline number. It's a partial sale plus a note plus continued exposure. Negotiate accordingly.

The single most common LOI mistake: signing the first one that comes in because the price looks good. The price is the easy part. The terms are where the deal lives or dies.

Have an M&A attorney review the LOI before you sign. Not your general counsel. Not your CPA. An actual M&A attorney. The fee is $2K–5K and it's the highest-ROI legal spend of your life.
