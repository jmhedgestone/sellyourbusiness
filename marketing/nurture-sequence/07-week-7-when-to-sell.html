Most owners I talk to want to sell after a bad year — when they're burned out, exhausted, or the business has hit a soft patch. That is, with painful regularity, the worst possible time.

Here's the framework I actually use to tell a client "list now" versus "wait 12 months":

✅ Three signals that say SELL NOW

  • Three years of stable or growing SDE. Not a hockey stick — just a consistent line. Buyers underwrite trends.
  • Industry multiples are at or above long-run averages. If comparable deals are closing at 4.5× and the historical norm is 3.5×, that's a 28% premium you bank by acting now.
  • You'd be embarrassed to show the books only because they're so good — not because they're a mess.

❌ Three signals that say WAIT

  • You're burned out. Burnout shows up in the numbers within 90 days and buyers will smell it. Hire a #2 first.
  • Last 12 months are softer than the prior 12. TTM-based valuations punish declining trends. Reverse the trend, then list.
  • One customer is >30% of revenue. Diversify first. Every percentage point you bring concentration down adds real dollars.

The single hardest thing I tell clients: sell from a position of strength, not exhaustion. The business is more valuable to a buyer when you don't urgently need to be done with it.

The exception: health events. Diagnosis, family emergency, partnership dispute. Fast sales for personal reasons happen all the time and that's fine — but go in clear-eyed about taking a 10–20% discount for speed. The market isn't cruel about it; it just prices the risk.

Calendar timing also matters more than people realize:

  • List in Jan-March. Closes happen 6 months later, in summer. Buyers don't want to take over a business in December.
  • Avoid election years if possible. Buyer activity drops 15–20% in the 90 days before a presidential election. Pricing is fine, deal velocity isn't.
  • Watch interest rates. Higher rates = harder for buyers to finance = lower multiples. Right now we're seeing some compression.
