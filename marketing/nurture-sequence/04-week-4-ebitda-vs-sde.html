Last week's email got more replies than any other I've sent — most of them asking the same thing:

"My business does $1.2M in earnings. Is that SDE or EBITDA?"

Different question than people realize. The answer determines your buyer pool, your multiple, and whether the broker is using the right valuation methodology in the first place.

The simple rule:

  • SDE includes the owner's salary. Use it for owner-operated businesses where one full-time owner is essential.
  • EBITDA excludes the owner's salary (treats it like a hired CEO's pay). Use it when the business is run by professional management — or could be.

Concrete example. Same business, two valuations:

Net income$700K
+ Owner salary ($200K market rate)$200K
+ Owner perks, D&A, interest, taxes$150K
SDE$1.05M
SDE minus market-rate owner salary−$200K
Adjusted EBITDA$850K

If this business is owner-operated, you sell on SDE: ~3.5× × $1.05M = $3.7M.
If it's run by professional management, you sell on EBITDA: ~5.5× × $850K = $4.7M.

Same business. Same cash flow. $1M difference — driven entirely by which methodology the buyer is using.

The cutoff most brokers use: under $1M earnings → SDE. Over $1M → EBITDA. Over $3M → strictly EBITDA, and now you're looking at private-equity buyers who pay even higher multiples.

Which means: if you're approaching $1M in earnings, the highest-leverage thing you can do isn't grow revenue another 20% — it's hire a general manager and shift to EBITDA-based valuation. The multiple expansion alone is usually 1–2 turns.
