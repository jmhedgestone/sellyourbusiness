Add-backs are where deals get made — or unmade. Get them right and your SDE jumps 20–40%, multiplied by the multiple. Get them wrong and the buyer walks during due diligence, accusing you of cooking the books.

Here's the spectrum I see on every deal:

✅ Add-backs buyers accept without a fight

  • Owner's W-2 salary (only on SDE-based valuations)
  • Owner's health insurance + retirement contributions
  • One-time legal fees (lawsuit, divorce, partnership buyout)
  • One-time IT or facility upgrades (new ERP, building remodel)
  • Depreciation & amortization (always)
  • Personal vehicle & fuel for the owner — IF documented

⚠️ Add-backs buyers will challenge (have proof)

  • Family members on payroll who don't actually work
  • Travel categorized as "business development"
  • Above-market rent paid to your own real estate LLC
  • Country club, season tickets, "client entertainment"
  • Owner's spouse's car

❌ Add-backs that kill deals

  • "Lost revenue from COVID" — buyers won't add back hypothetical revenue
  • Cash sales not on the books ("we did another $200K under the table")
  • Future cost savings the new owner would supposedly capture
  • Owner-funded marketing the buyer plans to keep doing
  • Anything you can't show on a credit card statement or W-2

The rule I tell every client: every add-back must be defensible with documentation. If the buyer's CPA asks "show me," you can pull it up in 30 seconds.

Real example: I lost a deal last year — $2.8M sale price, owner had $190K in "marketing expenses" he wanted added back. Couldn't produce invoices for $140K of it. Buyer's QoE firm flagged it, owner got defensive, deal died. He re-listed 8 months later, sold for $2.3M.

That's $500K vaporized over a clean spreadsheet.

If you're 12+ months from selling: start building an add-back tracker now. One spreadsheet, by month, with a screenshot or PDF of the invoice for each entry. When you go to market, you'll hand the buyer a clean defensible package and they'll pay the multiple.
