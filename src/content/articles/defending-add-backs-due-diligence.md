Most sellers learn what an add-back is, build a list, and assume the work is done. It isn't. [Knowing which add-backs are legitimate](/business-valuation/add-backs-business-valuation) is the easy half. The hard half is proving them to someone who is paid to be skeptical, at the exact moment you have the least leverage.

Here's how that conversation actually goes, and how to win it.

## The burden of proof is entirely yours

A buyer's accountant starts from a simple default: **every expense in your P&L is a real cost of running the business until you prove it isn't.**

That's not hostility. It's the only rational position — they're being asked to pay a multiple of a number you're adjusting upward. So the question is never "can they disprove your add-back?" It's "can you prove it?" An add-back you can't document isn't a negotiating position. It's a wish, and it comes out of the number.

## The three questions every add-back must survive

Each line gets tested against three things:

1. **Is it real and documented?** Can you produce a third-party record — an invoice, a statement, a payroll report?
2. **Is it genuinely personal or genuinely non-recurring?** Not "unusual." Not "we hope it won't happen again." Genuinely outside normal operations.
3. **Will it actually go away for the buyer?** This is the one sellers forget. If the new owner will still incur the cost, it isn't an add-back no matter how personal it felt to you.

That third question kills more add-backs than the other two combined. Your $60,000 salary for a family member is only an add-back if the business genuinely doesn't need that role filled. If the buyer has to hire someone to do what they were doing — even badly — the cost stays.

## What documentation actually looks like

Specificity is everything. Here's the standard of proof that holds up:

| Add-back | What actually proves it |
|---|---|
| Owner's vehicle | Lease or loan statements, insurance, usage records |
| Personal travel & meals | Itemized invoices and coded card detail, not a lump estimate |
| Family member on payroll | Payroll records plus honest evidence the role isn't needed |
| One-time legal matter | Engagement letter, invoices, settlement agreement, matter closed |
| Above-market related-party rent | The lease plus a market rent comparison or appraisal |
| One-time relocation or project | Dated vendor invoices tied to the specific event |
| Discretionary/charitable giving | Receipts and a record showing it was the owner's choice |

Notice the pattern: **contemporaneous and third-party**. A spreadsheet you built last month reconstructing three years of personal expenses from memory carries almost no weight — and it tells the buyer these adjustments weren't tracked when they happened.

## The add-backs that reliably die

After enough diligence processes, the casualty list is predictable:

**The "one-time" expense that happens every year.** The single most common strike. A one-time legal expense in 2024, 2025, and 2026 is not one-time — it's a recurring cost with a rotating explanation. Buyers pull the prior years specifically to catch this.

**Round numbers.** "Roughly $50,000 of personal expenses." Nothing announces an estimate like a round number, and estimates don't survive.

**Owner comp normalized the wrong way.** Adding back your full salary when the deal is priced on EBITDA rather than SDE. That's a [normalization error](/quality-of-earnings/normalized-adjusted-ebitda), and it's obvious to anyone who does this for a living.

**Deferred spending sold as improvement.** Maintenance you skipped and marketing you cut in the year before listing. Buyers look for this deliberately.

**Aggressive pro-forma projections.** Annualizing a good quarter. That's a forecast, and buyers don't pay multiples on forecasts.

## The credibility principle

Here's what sellers underestimate: **add-backs aren't evaluated independently. They're evaluated as a set.**

Put one obviously aggressive item on the schedule and you haven't just risked that line. You've told the buyer's accountant that your schedule needs auditing rather than reviewing. Lines they'd already accepted get reopened. The tone of the whole diligence shifts from verification to investigation.

Which leads to the counterintuitive move that makes sellers the most money: **concede your weak add-backs before the buyer finds them.**

Walking in and saying "we removed these two ourselves, they were marginal" does something no argument can. It establishes that your schedule is conservative, which means the rest of it is probably right. Buyers relax. Diligence speeds up. And the add-backs that actually matter sail through.

A tight, documented, slightly conservative schedule beats a padded one every single time — not because it's more virtuous, but because it nets more dollars.

## Build it before you list, not during diligence

Every add-back is worth its value times your multiple. A $50,000 line struck on a 4x business costs $200,000. That math is why this preparation pays better per hour than almost anything else you'll do.

And it can only be done early. Documentation has to be contemporaneous to be credible, which means the work starts 12 to 24 months before you go to market:

- Keep a running add-back schedule with support attached as expenses occur.
- Stop commingling now — every month of clean separation makes the next year's schedule easier. (See [getting your books diligence-ready](/quality-of-earnings/clean-books-diligence-ready-before-selling).)
- Have your broker or accountant challenge your own schedule before a buyer does. If it can't survive a friendly review, it will not survive a hostile one.

The sellers who defend their add-backs successfully aren't better arguers. They just did the work when there was still time to do it, and showed up with a folder instead of an explanation.

That process starts with an honest picture of what your recast earnings actually look like. A confidential valuation is the fastest way to find out — and to see which lines will hold when someone finally pushes.
