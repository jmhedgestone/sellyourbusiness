> This article is educational and is not tax or legal advice. Decisions about how income is reported — including any exposure for prior years — belong with your CPA and attorney.

Some version of this conversation happens in nearly every cash-heavy business sale. A restaurant, a laundromat, a car wash, a salon, a corner store. We're going through the financials, and the owner says some variation of:

*"The returns show $300,000. But the business really does $450,000. There's cash that doesn't run through the books."*

I understand exactly why owners do this, and I'm not here to lecture anyone about the past. But I owe every seller the truth about what it means for their sale, and the truth is blunt: **that extra $150,000 is worth zero at closing.** Not discounted. Zero.

Here's why — and what to do about it, because there is a legitimate answer that puts most of that money back in your pocket.

## You are asking someone to pay real money for a story

Put yourself on the other side of the table. A buyer is being asked to wire real, already-taxed dollars — plus, usually, a bank's money — for a business. You're telling them a third of the earnings exist but can't be shown on a return, a financial statement, or often even a deposit record.

There is nothing to check. No filing supports it. No accountant will attest to it. Even a buyer who *believes* you completely cannot act on that belief, because belief isn't collateral. They can't finance it, can't insure it, and — the part sellers miss — **can't resell the business on it later**. When their turn comes to exit, they'll face the same wall you're facing now.

So they do the only rational thing: they price the business on the earnings that can be proven, and treat the rest as a story.

## The lender closes the door completely

Even if you found a buyer willing to take your word, the financing usually isn't.

Most small business acquisitions depend on bank or SBA-backed loans, and that underwriting is built on **filed tax returns and prepared financial statements**. An underwriter cannot approve a loan against income the borrower's own filings say does not exist. There's no box for it.

The consequence is mechanical: if your provable earnings won't service the debt, the loan doesn't fund. And if the loan doesn't fund, your buyer pool collapses to people paying all cash — a much smaller group who, precisely because they're paying cash, expect a discount.

## A QofE won't rescue it either

If your buyer runs a [Quality of Earnings review](/quality-of-earnings/quality-of-earnings-report-explained), the analysis begins with proof of cash: tying reported revenue to bank deposits, merchant processing, and point-of-sale data.

That process is built to find exactly the gap you're describing — and it doesn't credit it. It surfaces it as a **risk**, not as upside. Reported revenue that doesn't reconcile to money actually arriving is one of the fastest ways to lose a buyer's confidence in every other number you've presented.

## And there's the part nobody says out loud

When you tell a buyer "we do another $150,000 in cash," you are telling a near-stranger, on the record, that you underreported your income to the government.

That's not a negotiating position. It's a disclosure — one that gives a buyer leverage over you for the rest of the transaction, and creates exposure that has nothing to do with the deal. Some buyers walk immediately, because a seller who was casual about their filings might be casual about other representations they're about to sign. Others stay, and remember it in every later negotiation.

I've never once seen it raise a price. I've seen it lower them.

## The legitimate fix — and the math that makes it worth doing

Here's the good news, and it's genuinely good: this problem is fixable, and fixing it usually makes you money.

**Start reporting all of your income, fully and consistently, before you go to market.** Buyers look at roughly three years of history, so two to three years of clean reporting is the practical target. Do that, and the income stops being a story. It becomes earnings — provable, financeable, and multiplied.

Run the numbers. Say $150,000 a year has been going unreported:

- **The cost:** you now pay tax on it. Depending on your bracket and state, call it roughly $50,000 a year. Over three years, about $150,000.
- **The gain:** your provable earnings rise by $150,000. At a 3x multiple, that's roughly **$450,000 of additional sale price.**

You spend around $150,000 in tax over three years to capture something in the neighborhood of $450,000 at closing — and you keep the underlying cash either way, since it was always your money. For most owners that's one of the highest-return decisions available in the entire sale process. (Your actual rates, multiple, and timing will differ — this is exactly the conversation to have with your CPA, including any question of prior-year exposure.)

The hardest part isn't the math. It's that the clock is long. This is a decision you make three years before you sell, which means it's a decision most owners realize they should have made about two years too late.

## If your business is legitimately cash-heavy

None of this is an argument against cash businesses. Plenty of restaurants, laundromats, and retailers report every dollar and sell perfectly well. But cash-intensive businesses face extra scrutiny regardless, simply because cash is harder to trace. Make it easy:

- **Run everything through the system.** Every sale through the POS, every dollar deposited. No exceptions and no side envelope.
- **Reconcile deposits to revenue monthly**, so the tie-out a buyer will perform is a formality rather than a discovery.
- **Keep the daily detail** — Z-reports, shift reports, merchant statements. Buyers want to see the pattern, not a monthly summary.
- **Expect the tie-out anyway.** Even with immaculate records, assume revenue will be traced to the bank. Being ready is the whole point of [getting your books diligence-ready](/quality-of-earnings/clean-books-diligence-ready-before-selling).

## The bottom line

You can only sell what you can prove. That sentence is the entire chapter.

Every owner in a cash business eventually faces the same choice: keep the tax savings, or build the sale price. You can't do both, because the very thing that lowers your tax bill is the thing that erases your earnings when it's time to sell. The multiple is what makes the choice lopsided — a dollar you don't report saves you cents, while a dollar you do report earns you several.

If you're two or three years out, you still have time to change the answer. If you're closer than that, the honest move is to price the business on what's provable and go to market with numbers that hold up.

Either way, start by finding out what your provable earnings are actually worth. A confidential valuation will tell you where you really stand — and whether the runway you have left is enough to change it.
