EBITDA is arithmetic. Any accountant can compute it from your statements and land on the same answer.

Adjusted EBITDA is something else entirely. It's a set of claims about what your business *would* earn under a normal owner — and every one of those claims has to be defended. This is where the real money in a deal is won or lost, because the number that comes out the end gets multiplied.

## The bridge, one step at a time

Normalization runs in two stages: first you get to EBITDA, then you normalize it.

**Stage one — mechanical.** Start with net income and add back the four items the acronym names: interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization. The logic is that these reflect *your* capital structure and *your* tax situation, not the underlying economics of the business. A buyer will have their own debt and their own tax profile.

**Stage two — judgment.** Now restate the result to reflect the business running normally under someone else. This is where the arguments live:

**Owner compensation, reset to market.** This is the most misunderstood adjustment on the list. In [SDE, you add back the owner's entire compensation](/business-valuation/what-is-sde-sellers-discretionary-earnings) — that metric measures the whole benefit to one working owner. In EBITDA, you do *not*. You normalize to what a replacement manager would cost. Pay yourself $400,000 for a job the market fills at $150,000, and the adjustment is the $250,000 difference. Get this backwards and you'll overstate your business by a mile. (Which metric applies to you is covered in [EBITDA vs SDE](/business-valuation/ebitda-vs-sde-business-valuation).)

**Related-party rent, reset to market.** If you own the building and charge the business $180,000 for space that rents for $120,000, the extra $60,000 isn't a business expense — it's you moving money to yourself. It gets added back. It cuts the other way too: charge yourself nothing and a buyer will *deduct* market rent, because they'll have to pay it. This is one of the sharpest edges for owner-occupants, and it's why the [property and the business get valued separately](/real-estate/valuing-business-and-real-estate-separately).

**Genuinely non-recurring items.** A lawsuit settled once. A flood. A single office relocation. The word doing the work here is *genuinely* — more on that below.

**Personal and discretionary expenses.** The vehicles, the travel that was really a vacation, the family members on payroll who don't work there. Legitimate, but only with documentation.

**Non-operating income and expenses.** Investment income, gains on asset sales, anything unrelated to running the business. Strip it out — buyers aren't buying it.

**Accounting timing.** Cash-basis books get restated to an accrual view. Revenue recorded in the wrong period gets moved. Deferred revenue, prepaid expenses, and inventory get corrected. This isn't cheating — it's making the earnings comparable to how a buyer will run the books.

## What it looks like in practice

An illustrative bridge for a business with $800,000 of net income:

| Line | Amount |
|---|---|
| Net income | $800,000 |
| + Interest | $60,000 |
| + Depreciation & amortization | $140,000 |
| **= EBITDA** | **$1,000,000** |
| + Owner comp above market ($400k paid − $150k market) | $250,000 |
| + Related-party rent above market ($180k paid − $120k market) | $60,000 |
| + One-time litigation settlement | $75,000 |
| + Documented personal expenses | $45,000 |
| **= Adjusted EBITDA** | **$1,430,000** |

At a 5x multiple, that's the difference between a $5.0M business and a $7.15M business. The adjustments are worth **$2.15 million** of purchase price.

Now you understand why a buyer's accountant reads each line so carefully — and why "trust me" is not a documentation strategy. (These are illustrative figures; your multiple depends on your industry and risk profile — see [multiples by industry](/business-valuation/business-valuation-multiples-by-industry).)

## The adjustments that draw fire

Some categories reliably invite a fight:

**"One-time" items that keep happening.** A one-time legal expense in three consecutive years is not a one-time legal expense. It's a cost of doing business, and a QofE will say so.

**Pro-forma and run-rate adjustments.** Annualizing a price increase from month nine, or projecting a full year for a location opened in month seven. Sellers love these; buyers resist them, because they're forecasts dressed as history. They can survive if the change is documented, already implemented, and clearly durable — but they attract the most scrutiny of anything on the list.

**Deferred spending disguised as margin improvement.** Skipping maintenance, cutting marketing, or leaving a role unfilled in the year before a sale improves EBITDA on paper. Buyers look for exactly this, and finding it damages your credibility on every other adjustment.

**Growth capex mislabeled as one-time.** If the business needs regular investment to sustain its earnings, that spending isn't an add-back — it's the price of the earnings.

## The trailing twelve months, and the trap of a great last year

Adjusted EBITDA is usually measured on a trailing twelve-month basis, because it's the freshest read. But buyers will line it up against the prior two or three fiscal years.

A TTM figure that's dramatically better than the two years before it doesn't get taken at face value. It gets interrogated. If the improvement is real — a genuine new contract, a durable price increase, a structural cost change — document it, and it will hold. If it's the product of one soft year of spending, it won't, and the attempt will cost you goodwill on the adjustments that were legitimate.

## The real lesson

Every dollar of adjusted EBITDA is worth a multiple of itself. That cuts both ways: it's why doing this work carefully pays so well, and why doing it aggressively backfires so badly. Conservative, documented, defensible adjustments hold up and get multiplied. Aggressive ones get struck — and they take your credibility with them.

The place to build that bridge is 12 to 24 months before you go to market, not in week six of due diligence. A confidential valuation is the fastest way to see what your normalized number actually looks like today, and what it would take to move it.
