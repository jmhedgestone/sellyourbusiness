There's a moment in most deals that sellers never see coming. You've agreed on a price. You've signed a Letter of Intent. You've taken the business off the market and stopped returning calls from other buyers. And then a team of accountants you've never met spends six weeks inside your financials and produces a report saying your earnings aren't what you said they were.

That report is a Quality of Earnings analysis. Understanding what it is — and what it looks for — is the difference between defending your price and watching it get cut.

## What a QofE actually is

A Quality of Earnings report is an independent accounting analysis that answers one question: **are these earnings real, recurring, and transferable to a new owner?**

It's performed by an accounting or transaction advisory firm, and it is emphatically **not an audit**. That distinction matters more than most sellers realize:

- An **audit** asks whether your financial statements are fairly presented under accounting standards. It looks backward at a fiscal year and produces a formal opinion.
- A **QofE** asks what a buyer is actually purchasing. It ignores compliance formality and rebuilds your earnings from the underlying data — usually on a trailing twelve-month basis — to isolate the profit that will still be there next year under someone else's ownership.

A business with spotless audited financials can still get an ugly QofE. The audit says the numbers were recorded correctly. The QofE says whether those numbers mean what the seller claims they mean.

## What they actually dig into

A QofE team goes well past the P&L. Expect them to examine:

**Revenue quality.** Not just how much, but what kind. Is it recurring or one-off? Contracted or at-will? How much comes from a handful of customers? Revenue concentrated in two accounts is worth less than the same revenue spread across two hundred.

**Proof of cash.** They tie reported revenue to bank deposits, merchant processing, and point-of-sale data. Revenue that doesn't reconcile to money actually arriving is the fastest way to lose a buyer's trust.

**Margin trends.** A margin that jumped last year invites the question of whether it's a real improvement or deferred spending — maintenance you skipped, marketing you cut, a hire you didn't make to dress up the numbers before selling.

**Every add-back you've claimed.** Each one gets tested for whether it's genuinely non-recurring, genuinely personal, and genuinely documented. This is where most value gets lost, and it's why [defending your add-backs](/quality-of-earnings/defending-add-backs-due-diligence) deserves its own preparation.

**Related-party dealings.** Rent paid to yourself for a building you own, your spouse on payroll, a supplier you have an interest in — all restated to market terms. (If you own your building, this connects directly to [valuing the property separately](/real-estate/valuing-business-and-real-estate-separately).)

**Working capital.** How much cash the business genuinely needs to operate, and whether the level you're delivering at closing is normal or manipulated.

**Accounting timing.** Cash-basis books get converted to an accrual view. Revenue booked in the wrong period gets moved. Prepaid expenses, deferred revenue, and inventory all get scrutinized.

## Buy-side versus sell-side — and why the timing is everything

A **buy-side QofE** is commissioned by the buyer, after the LOI, during exclusivity. By then you've taken the business off the market, told yourself the deal is done, and lost your alternatives. Anything the report finds becomes leverage — and the standard outcome is a **retrade**: a request to lower the price based on the findings.

A **sell-side QofE** is one you commission on your own business, before you list. Same rigor, opposite dynamic. You find the weak add-back, the concentration issue, or the accrual problem while you still have time to fix it, explain it, or price it in. You hand buyers a pre-vetted earnings picture, which shortens diligence and makes your number far harder to argue with.

The work is identical. The only variable is who finds the problem first, and whether you're negotiating from strength or from a corner.

## What it costs — and what it's compared against

Cost scales with the size and messiness of the business. For lower-middle-market deals, a sell-side QofE commonly runs into the tens of thousands of dollars; larger or more complicated companies cost considerably more.

That's real money, and for a smaller business it may not be justified. But the comparison isn't "QofE versus zero." It's "QofE versus a retrade." If a buyer's accountant strikes $100,000 of add-backs on a business trading at 4x, that's $400,000 of purchase price — many times the cost of finding it yourself first.

## Do you actually need one?

It depends on your size and your buyer.

**You probably do** if your business is EBITDA-scale, if private equity is a likely buyer (they'll run one regardless — see our [private equity guide](/private-equity)), if your financials are complex or have related-party dealings, or if you've had unusual events in the last three years.

**You probably don't** — at least not a formal one — if you're a smaller, SDE-based business selling to an individual buyer. But don't mistake that for an absence of scrutiny. The buyer's CPA will still tie your revenue to deposits, still question your add-backs, and still notice that last year's "one-time" legal expense also appeared the year before. The review is less formal; the standard isn't.

Either way, the preparation is the same. The businesses that sail through diligence aren't the ones with perfect numbers — they're the ones whose owners already knew where the soft spots were and had answers ready.

That work starts with an honest picture of what the business earns and what it's worth. A confidential valuation is the right first step, and the sooner you take it, the more time you have to fix what it surfaces.
